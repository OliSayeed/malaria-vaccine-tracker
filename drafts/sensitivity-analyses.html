Sensitivity Analyses — Modelling Assumptions

For each modelling choice, this document quantifies how much each country's estimate moves under alternative parameter settings. Baseline is the seasonal model with Average completion, 6-month rollout, 48-month protection window. 21 countries with matched shipment + seasonality + burden data.

Key insight: Most modelling assumptions (completion rates, vaccine choice, protection window, 3-dose efficacy) act as uniform multipliers on cases averted — they shift every country by the same percentage. For these, "which countries matter most?" is simply "which countries have the largest baseline estimates" (Uganda, Mozambique, Burkina Faso, Ghana, Nigeria).

Only three assumptions produce country-specific differences: rollout duration (matters for highly seasonal countries), seasonality data source (matters where incidence and entomological profiles diverge), and the population denominator for incidence (matters where age structure differs from the regional norm).

Uniform-scaling assumptions

Affect all 21 countries by the same percentage. The biggest absolute impacts fall on Uganda, Mozambique, Burkina Faso, Ghana, and Nigeria, simply because they have the largest baseline estimates.
  • Completion scenario: Optimistic to Pessimistic range = 94% of average
  • Vaccine choice: All-R21 gives 33.9% more cases averted than all-RTS,S
  • 3-dose efficacy: Dropping 3-dose protection cuts estimate by 32.6%
  • Protection window: 3 years = 80% of 4 years; 5 years = 117% of 4 years

Country-differentiating assumptions

Affect countries by different amounts depending on local factors.
  • Rollout duration: Mali shifts ±0.86%, Zambia ±0.55%; low-seasonality countries ±0%
  • Seasonality source: Malawi shifts 0.62% when using entomological data; most countries <0.3%
  • Population denominator: Under-5 scaling varies from 5.4× (Niger, Chad) to 8.0× (Ghana, Kenya)

Sensitivity 1 · Completion rate scenario Uniform

Range: Optimistic gives 47% more cases averted than Average; Pessimistic gives 47% fewer. Total spread = ~94% of central estimate. The ratio is essentially identical across countries because the same completion rates are applied to every country. Countries with the largest dose volumes have the largest absolute uncertainty: Uganda alone spans ~660,000 cases across scenarios.

Sensitivity 2 · Vaccine choice (RTS,S vs R21 counterfactual) Uniform

Counterfactual: estimate cases averted if each country's entire programme had been delivered with all-RTS,S or all-R21. R21 produces 33.9% more cases averted than RTS,S across every country — the same ratio applies regardless of seasonality, geography, or burden. This is driven entirely by the efficacy gap (R21: 75% → 48% over 4 years vs RTS,S: 56% → 36%). Mixed countries (Burkina Faso, Ghana) sit between the two extremes today.

Sensitivity 3 · Protection window (3y / 4y / 5y) Uniform

How does cases averted change if we model a 3-year or 5-year protection window instead of 4 years? 3 years gives 80% of the baseline; 5 years gives 117% — uniform across all countries because the efficacy curve shape is the same. The 4y → 5y extrapolation relies on exponential decay beyond trial data.

Sensitivity 4 · 3-dose efficacy assumption Uniform

Currently, children completing 3 doses but not 4 are assumed to receive the 4-dose efficacy curve shifted forward 12 months (i.e., they start at the year-2 level on the curve). What if we instead assumed 3-dose children get no protection (only counting 4-dose completers)? The estimate drops by ~32.6% uniformly — because the share of doses reaching dose 3 but not dose 4 is the same in every country under the Average completion scenario (21.6% of children). This is the most consequential single assumption that affects all countries equally.

Sensitivity 5 · Rollout duration (6 vs 12 months) Country-differentiating

Lengthening the rollout from 6 months to 12 months averages each shipment's protection over a wider range of seasonal months, pulling the seasonal multiplier closer to 1 (the flat estimate). This effect is large only where seasonality is strong AND shipments were timed close to optimal/pessimal months. Mali (R = 0.37, March shipments to October-peak country) loses 0.86% of its estimate when rollout is doubled; countries with R < 0.1 are essentially unaffected.

Sensitivity 6 · Seasonality data source (incidence vs entomological) Country-differentiating

For 15 of 21 countries, we have both incidence-based and entomological seasonality profiles. The baseline uses incidence (closer to disease burden). Entomological profiles measure vector activity and often peak earlier and more sharply. Switching sources shifts each country's estimate by typically <0.3%, but Malawi shifts by 0.62% because its entomological R (0.58) is far larger than its incidence R (0.22). Chad and Mozambique also have large R-gaps. For these countries, the choice of which seasonality dataset to trust is a meaningful modelling decision.

Sensitivity 7 · Population denominator for incidence Country-differentiating

Baseline computes annual cases per person using the full population-at-risk denominator. But the vaccinated population is under-5 children, and most malaria cases in Africa fall in under-5s. As an upper-bound counterfactual: what if we used the under-5 population as the denominator instead? The scaling factor varies from 5.4× (Niger, Chad — high birth rate, young populations) to 8.0× (Ghana, Kenya — older populations). This is a country-specific demographic effect: countries with proportionally fewer under-5s would see larger upward revisions. Note: this is illustrative only — the true correction should weight by the actual age-distribution of malaria cases (estimated at ~60–75% in under-5s in high-burden settings), not 100%.
