Methodology

What the adjustment does. The standard model treats malaria incidence as uniform across months, multiplying doses by an annual average case rate. The seasonal adjustment weights each month of protection by how much malaria actually circulates in that month. A dose landing before the peak season protects children when transmission is highest, so it averts more cases than the flat model predicts — and vice versa.

Seasonal multiplier formula. For a shipment arriving in calendar month M (0 = January), using vaccine v with efficacy curve e(t) over a 48-month protection window, and monthly transmission shares share[0..11] (normalised to sum to 1):

multiplier(v, M) = 12 × Σ_{t=0}^{47} [ e(t) × share[(M + t) mod 12] ]
                         ─────────────────────────────────────────────
                               Σ_{t=0}^{47} e(t)

A multiplier of 1.05 means the seasonal model predicts 5% more cases averted than the flat model; 0.99 means 1% fewer.

Efficacy curves. Piecewise linear between trial data points (year 1, 2.67, 4), with exponential decay thereafter. RTS,S: 56% → 44% → 36%. R21: 75% → 58.9% → 48.2%. Flat at initial value before year 1.

Actual adjustment column. Each country's shipments are multiplied by their individual multipliers; the result is dose-weighted to produce a single country-level figure. This reflects the realised effect of when shipments actually arrived.

Best / Worst possible columns. The multiplier is computed for all 12 possible starting months and the extremes are reported. This shows the maximum benefit that could have been achieved by timing shipments optimally, and the maximum cost of poor timing.

Seasonality index R (Mean Resultant Length). A circular-statistics measure of how concentrated transmission is. Computed as:

C = Σ_{i=0}^{11} share[i] × cos(2πi/12)
S = Σ_{i=0}^{11} share[i] × sin(2πi/12)
R = √(C² + S²)

R = 0 means perfectly flat year-round; R = 1 means all transmission falls in a single month. Countries with R < 0.1 (e.g. Cameroon, Uganda, Côte d'Ivoire) are near-perennial and nearly insensitive to timing. Countries with R > 0.3 (e.g. Mali, Niger, Zambia) show the largest timing effects.

Seasonality profiles. Derived from Seasonality_Dataset_Revised.xlsx. Monthly incidence values from each sub-national study are normalised to shares summing to 1, then averaged across studies within each country. Incidence profiles are preferred; entomological profiles are used only where incidence data are unavailable.

Limitations. (1) The efficacy curve used is for 4-dose completers; the dose-dropout cascade is not modelled. (2) Linear rollout ramp (6 months) is not modelled; the shipment date is treated as the start of immunisation. Both simplifications are consistent with the standalone seasonal-adjustment draft. (3) Four countries (DRC, Sierra Leone, Central African Republic, Guinea-Bissau) have shipment data but no matching seasonality profile and are excluded.
