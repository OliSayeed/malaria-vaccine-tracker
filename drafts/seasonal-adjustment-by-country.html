Methodology

What the adjustment does. The standard model treats malaria incidence as uniform across months. The seasonal adjustment weights each month of protection by how much malaria actually circulates that month. A child receiving dose 3 just before peak transmission will have more cases averted than the flat estimate; one immunised at trough will have fewer.

Why the first year of protection is invariant. The efficacy curve is flat at its initial value for the first 12 months post-dose-3. Because cases averted = efficacy × transmission × time, and the first year always covers exactly one complete annual cycle (monthly shares sum to 1), the year-1 contribution is the same regardless of when protection starts. The entire timing effect therefore comes from the declining years 2–4, where you want peak-transmission months to encounter the highest remaining efficacy. This is why the effects are modest (±0.5–1.5% for most countries) even where seasonality is strong.

Optimal timing implication. For a single autumn peak (e.g. September), the best dose-3 month is approximately July–August — just before the peak — so that the peak also falls early in the declining years (months 12, 24, 36 of the curve). Shipping 4–5 months before peak achieves this after the rollout + dose-spacing lag.

Corrected multiplier formula. Delivery in calendar month M, vaccine efficacy curve e(t) over a 48-month window, monthly transmission shares s[0..11] summing to 1, dose-1-to-dose-3 lag = 2 months, rollout = 6 months:

multiplier(M) = (1/6) × Σ_{k=0}^{5} mult_base(M + 2 + k)

where  mult_base(start) = 12 × Σ_{t=0}^{47} [ e(t) × s[(start + t) mod 12] ]
                                ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                              Σ_{t=0}^{47} e(t)

The average is taken uniformly over the 6 possible dose-3 start months, reflecting the linear rollout assumption that dose 1 is delivered equally across months M to M+5.

Efficacy curves (post-dose-3). Piecewise linear between trial points, exponential decay beyond year 4. Flat at initial value before year 1.

Actual adjustment column. Each shipment's multiplier is computed individually (using its actual delivery date); the country figure is the dose-weighted average across all its shipments. The greyed naive figure shows what the same calculation gives if rollout and dose timing are ignored (delivery month = protection start), to illustrate how much the correction matters.

Limitations. (1) Dose-dropout cascade is not modelled; all doses are treated as 4-dose completers. (2) Dose-4 timing (12 months after dose 3) is implicitly captured in the efficacy curve used. (3) Four countries (DRC, Sierra Leone, Central African Republic, Guinea-Bissau) have no matching seasonality profile and are excluded.
